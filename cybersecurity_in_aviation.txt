# Cybersecurity in Aviation: Exploring the Significance, Applications, and Challenges of Cybersecurity in the Aviation Sector

**Authors:** Mahmoud Eleimat, Arnold Őszi  
**Journal:** *Periodica Polytechnica Transportation Engineering*, 53(2), pp. 169–183 (2025)  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.3311/PPtr.37153  
**Date read:** 2026-06-20

---

## Executive Summary

This review investigates the growing importance of cybersecurity within the aviation sector as digital transformation increases the interconnectivity of aircraft, airports, air traffic management systems, and passenger services.

The authors synthesize current threats, emerging technologies, major application domains, and implementation challenges affecting aviation cybersecurity. The paper argues that cybersecurity must be treated as a core aviation safety requirement rather than a secondary IT concern.

The central premise is that modern aviation increasingly resembles a cyber-physical ecosystem where disruptions to digital infrastructure can create cascading operational, financial, and safety consequences.

---

## Research Objectives

The paper addresses four major questions:

1. Why is cybersecurity essential for modern aviation?
2. What cyber threats currently affect aviation systems?
3. Which cybersecurity applications are most critical for aviation?
4. What challenges limit effective cybersecurity implementation?

---

## Why Cybersecurity Matters in Aviation

Modern aviation depends on interconnected digital systems across:

- Aircraft avionics and flight control systems
- Air traffic management (ATM)
- Airport operations
- Passenger reservation systems
- Cargo logistics
- In-flight entertainment systems
- Communication and navigation infrastructure

A successful cyberattack may affect:

- Flight safety
- Passenger privacy
- Operational continuity
- National security
- Economic stability
- Critical infrastructure interdependencies

The paper emphasizes that cyber incidents in aviation can create cascading impacts across finance, healthcare, telecommunications, transportation, energy, and government sectors.

---

## Threat Landscape

The authors organize cyber risks around the **CIA triad**:

- **Confidentiality**
- **Integrity**
- **Availability**

Based on a review of aviation incidents from 2001–2021:

- **71%** targeted login credentials and unauthorized access
- **25%** involved denial-of-service attacks
- **4%** focused on integrity violations and data manipulation

### Major Threat Sources

- Nation-state actors
- Terrorist organizations
- Cybercriminal groups
- Insider threats
- Supply-chain vulnerabilities
- Legacy system weaknesses

---

## Real-World Examples Discussed

- Al-Qaeda plots targeting aviation infrastructure
- Operation Overt (2006)
- Operation Hemorrhage (2010)
- Malaysia Airlines website breach (2015)
- Research demonstrating potential aircraft hijacking via mobile platforms

These cases illustrate that aviation cyberattacks may pursue:

- Financial disruption
- Reputational damage
- Operational paralysis
- Physical harm

## Core Application Areas

### 1. Air Traffic Management (ATM) Security

Key focus areas:

- Secure communication channels
- System-wide information management (SWIM)
- ADS-B protection
- Trajectory management security
- NextGen and SESAR cybersecurity integration

Key insight:

ATM modernization increases efficiency but expands the attack surface.

### 2. Blockchain in Aviation

Potential use cases:

- Passenger identity management
- Passenger name records (PNR)
- Advance passenger information (API)
- Ticketing systems
- Loyalty programs
- Aircraft maintenance records
- Supply chain traceability
- Aircraft leasing contracts

Benefits:

- Immutability
- Transparency
- Reduced intermediaries
- Improved auditability

Limitations:

- Jurisdictional uncertainty
- Privacy concerns
- Regulatory complexity

### 3. Smart Airports

Airport evolution model:

- Airport 1.0: Basic operations
- Airport 2.0: Agile digital integration
- Airport 3.0: Smart IoT-enabled infrastructure

Reported distribution:

- 16% Basic
- 56% Agile
- 28% Smart

Critical cybersecurity requirements:

- Access control
- IoT security
- Continuous monitoring
- Threat intelligence sharing
- Employee awareness

### 4. Aircraft Systems Protection

Recommended controls:

- Secure-by-design architectures
- Network segmentation
- Intrusion detection and prevention systems (IDPS)
- Patch management
- Multifactor authentication
- Encryption
- Threat modeling
- Penetration testing

## What Is New in This Paper

- Provides an integrated overview of aviation cybersecurity spanning aircraft, airports, ATM systems, and emerging technologies.
- Connects aviation safety engineering with cybersecurity risk management.
- Synthesizes cybersecurity applications across the entire aviation ecosystem rather than focusing on a single subsystem.
- Highlights blockchain as an emerging cybersecurity enabler for aviation operations.
- Introduces the smart airport maturity model as a lens for evaluating cybersecurity preparedness.
- Consolidates aviation cyberattack statistics into the CIA framework.
- Frames cybersecurity as a strategic challenge involving technology, regulation, economics, and workforce development.

---

## Key Insights for Researchers

### Cybersecurity is now a safety issue

The distinction between IT security and operational safety is disappearing.

Future work should integrate:

- Safety engineering
- Cyber risk assessment
- Resilience modeling

### Legacy systems remain the weakest link

Critical aviation systems often lack:

- Encryption
- Authentication
- Secure update mechanisms

Modernization must address security debt.

### Interconnectivity creates systemic risk

Smart airports, IoT deployments, and digital ATM systems increase attack surfaces.

Researchers should prioritize:

- Zero-trust architectures
- Secure interoperability
- Network segmentation

### Human factors are underexplored

Major challenges include:

- Workforce shortages
- Insider threats
- Cybersecurity training

Human-centered security deserves greater attention.

### Collaboration is essential

Effective aviation cybersecurity requires:

- Public-private partnerships
- International standards
- Cross-border information sharing

- Secure integration of AI into ATM systems
- Blockchain governance frameworks
- Digital twin security
- Resilient ADS-B alternatives
- Cyber-physical risk models
- Automated threat intelligence sharing

- Explainable AI for aviation cybersecurity

| Passenger identity management | Blockchain | Trusted data sharing |
| Airport operations | IoT security platforms | Operational resilience |
| ATM modernization | SWIM + secure communications | Safer coordination |
| Insider threats | Training and monitoring | Reduced human risk |
| Legacy systems | Network segmentation | Attack containment |

---

## Key Takeaway

The paper argues that aviation cybersecurity should evolve from reactive protection toward proactive cyber resilience.

Future aviation systems must be designed as secure cyber-physical ecosystems where safety, security, privacy, and operational efficiency are jointly optimized.

Long-term success will depend on combining technological innovation with regulatory harmonization, workforce development, and international collaboration.

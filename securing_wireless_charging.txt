# Executive Summary

This paper investigates the cybersecurity implications of integrating **Open Charge Point Protocol (OCPP)-based wireless charging infrastructures** into **Intelligent Transportation Systems (ITS)**.

The authors argue that as wireless power transfer (WPT) evolves toward dynamic charging and autonomous transport ecosystems, cybersecurity vulnerabilities within charging communication layers become critical operational risks.

To quantify these risks, the study introduces a **Stochastic Petri Net (SPN)-based risk assessment framework** that maps vulnerability severity scores directly onto operational service states.

Using the **intelligent Bus on Campus (iBuC)** platform as a case study, the authors evaluate how vulnerabilities in electric vehicle charging networks affect broader transportation services.

The study demonstrates that:

* Wireless charging infrastructure significantly expands the ITS attack surface.
* OCPP vulnerabilities can propagate into transport service disruptions.
* Protocol-level attacks may translate into physical-layer failures in wireless charging scenarios.
* Security assessments must account for both operational states and charging subprocesses.

# Research Problem

Existing ITS cybersecurity research often evaluates:

* Vehicle-to-Infrastructure (V2I) security
* Autonomous vehicle communication
* Charging infrastructure security

However, limited work examines:

1. How charging vulnerabilities affect overall transport services.
2. How wireless charging alters exploitation dynamics.
3. How third-party IoT integrations influence charging security.
4. How protocol-level weaknesses propagate through cyber-physical systems.

The paper addresses these gaps by linking:

**Vulnerabilities → Operational States → Service Reliability**

# Methodology

## Stochastic Petri Nets (SPNs)

The authors model ITS behavior using SPNs because they:

* Capture asynchronous events.
* Represent distributed IoT environments.
* Model state transitions probabilistically.
* Support timing analysis.

SPNs enable evaluation of:

* State occupancy probabilities
* Service life cycles
* Attack exposure windows
* Security impacts over time

1. Relevance to operational states.
2. CVSS severity.

# Security Metric Formulation

The framework computes:

* Frequency of occurrence (Rg)
* Severity (Wg)
* Risk allocation (Pg)

These are aggregated into:

* **SMVL(0):** Baseline risk metric
* **SMVL(t):** Mitigated risk metric

Interpretation:

* Higher values indicate greater risk density.
* Lower values indicate more resilient state distributions.

Importantly:

The metric represents **relative risk density**, not absolute attack count.

---

# Important Interpretation

A lower SMEVL score **does not necessarily mean lower overall threat exposure**.

Instead, it indicates:

* Reduced risk concentration per state.
* Wider distribution of vulnerabilities across the system.

Researchers should therefore evaluate:

1. Risk density metrics.
2. Total vulnerability count.
3. State occupancy probabilities.

Together.

# Wireless Charging-Specific Insights

The paper highlights a critical distinction:

Traditional plug-in charging tolerates communication delays.

Wireless charging does not.

Dynamic wireless charging requires:

* Microsecond-scale authentication
* Real-time synchronization
* Continuous OCPP communication

Consequently:

Protocol attacks can directly cause physical disruptions.

Examples include:

* Authentication delays
* Resource exhaustion attacks
* Signal jamming
* Foreign Object Detection (FOD) spoofing
* Electromagnetic interference

These attacks can terminate inductive power transfer and trigger Denial-of-Service conditions.

# Key Exposure Findings

PEV vulnerabilities affect:

* **43%** of operational states in iBuC-PTS.
* **33%** of operational states in iBuC-WFS.

The charging process therefore influences nearly half of the service lifecycle.

# Notable Example

The probability of remaining in the charging-related state increases:

* From **0.35** in the public transport scenario
* To **0.50** in the weather integration scenario

Implication:

Third-party IoT integrations can unintentionally increase charging attack exposure windows.

# Design Recommendations

The study recommends:

* Migrating to OCPP 2.0.1 security profiles.
* Enforcing TLS-based communication.
* Using certificate-based authentication.
* Securing firmware update channels.
* Implementing high-speed authentication.
* Monitoring charging state occupancy.
* Integrating physical-layer security mechanisms.
* Combining OCPP protections with wireless pad authentication.

# Limitations

The authors acknowledge that:

* OCPP vulnerabilities are still emerging.
* Legacy deployments remain widespread.
* Historical CVEs may not fully represent future threats.
* Dynamic charging environments require further study.

Additionally:

The framework evaluates relative risk rather than absolute exploit probability.

# How secure are your health devices—stopping wearables becoming a personal and national security risk?

**Authors:** Max Ostermann, Oscar Freyer, Carsten Weinhold, Kai Martius, Stephen Gilbert

**Journal:** *npj Digital Medicine* (News & Views), Volume 8, Article 317 (2025)  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s41746-025-01710-2  
**Date Read:** 2026-06-20

---

## Executive Summary

This article argues that cybersecurity risks in the Internet of Medical Things (IoMT) have shifted from isolated software vulnerabilities toward systemic supply-chain threats involving hardware, firmware, cloud services, and geopolitical dependencies.

The authors highlight how modern healthcare increasingly depends on wearable and connected medical devices deployed in remote patient monitoring (RPM) and Hospital-at-Home settings. As these devices become cheaper, more connected, and globally sourced, the attack surface expands dramatically.

The paper emphasizes that cybersecurity is no longer only a patient privacy issue—it is a patient safety, healthcare resilience, and national security issue.

---

## Research Context

The article is motivated by three converging trends:

- Rapid growth of IoMT and wearable health devices
- Expansion of remote patient monitoring ecosystems
- Increasing concern over supply-chain attacks and hardware backdoors

The authors examine how geopolitical tensions, globalized manufacturing, and opaque supply chains create new security risks that existing medical device regulations only partially address.

---

## Core Argument

The primary cybersecurity challenge has evolved:

- **Past focus:** software bugs and insecure network services
- **Current focus:** compromised supply chains, firmware manipulation, and hardware backdoors

Modern IoMT devices depend on:

- Third-party chip manufacturers
- Software vendors
- Cloud providers
- Wireless communication modules
- International logistics networks

As a result, healthcare organizations inherit security risks they often cannot observe or audit.

## Historical Examples

### Implantable Cardiac Devices

The article revisits the evolution of cybersecurity concerns surrounding implantable cardioverter-defibrillators (ICDs).

Key milestones include:

- 2008: First peer-reviewed demonstration of ICD vulnerabilities
- 2012: Popular awareness through the *Homeland* television series
- 2016–2017: FDA acknowledgment of vulnerabilities in St. Jude Medical devices
- First cybersecurity-related recall affecting approximately 500,000 devices

Similar vulnerabilities have been demonstrated in:

- Insulin pumps
- Connected cardiac implants
- Remote monitoring systems

## What Is New in This Paper

- Reframes IoMT cybersecurity as a **supply-chain security problem** rather than solely a software security problem.
- Connects wearable device security to broader geopolitical and national security concerns.
- Highlights recent evidence of hardware backdoors in medical devices.
- Demonstrates how Hospital-at-Home programs introduce new attack surfaces.
- Integrates regulatory, technical, and supply-chain perspectives into a single framework.
- Emphasizes hardware Roots of Trust and Zero Trust architectures as foundational design principles.
- Argues that healthcare cybersecurity must extend beyond device approval into lifecycle monitoring.

## Key Findings

### 1. IoMT ecosystems have fundamentally changed

Compared with 2015, connected medical devices are now:

- Cheaper
- More disposable
- More reliant on Bluetooth connectivity
- More dependent on consumer electronics ecosystems
- Increasingly manufactured through globally distributed supply chains

### 2. Supply-chain attacks are realistic

Medical devices may contain:

- Compromised firmware
- Malicious code
- Vulnerable communication modules
- Hardware backdoors

These vulnerabilities may be introduced long before deployment.

### 3. Hardware vulnerabilities can create catastrophic outcomes

Potential consequences include:

- Suppressing emergency alarms
- Manipulating patient data
- Preventing clinical intervention
- Enabling remote device control
- Compromising hospital networks

### 4. Current regulation remains incomplete

Existing frameworks include:

- EU Medical Device Regulation (MDR)
- EU Cyber Resilience Act (CRA)
- US Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (FD&C Act)

However, the authors argue that regulatory requirements remain fragmented and insufficiently explicit.

## Technical Mitigations

### Root of Trust

Hardware-level trust anchors include:

- Trusted Platform Modules (TPMs)
- eSIMs
- Secure cryptographic key storage

Benefits:

- Device attestation
- Integrity verification
- Secure boot processes

### Secure-by-Design

Key principles:

- Principle of Least Authority
- Software isolation
- Minimal privileges
- Formal verification

### Zero Trust Architecture

Core elements:

- Continuous authentication
- Network segmentation
- Least-privilege access
- Continuous monitoring

### Supply-Chain Visibility

Recommended approaches:

- Component traceability
- Software Bill of Materials (SBOM)
- Automated risk detection
- Supplier verification

## Research Insights

### Cybersecurity is becoming a geopolitical issue

Device security increasingly depends on:

- Semiconductor independence
- Trusted vendors
- Procurement policies
- National supply-chain resilience

### Hardware security is underexplored

Most research focuses on:

- Software vulnerabilities
- Encryption
- Network security

Future work should investigate:

- Chip-level security
- Firmware integrity
- Hardware attestation

### Home healthcare changes the threat model

Hospital-at-Home programs extend clinical infrastructure into:

- Residential networks
- Consumer routers
- Personal devices

Researchers must develop security models specifically for decentralized care.

### Supply-chain transparency is a major research gap

Critical questions include:

- How can suppliers be audited?
- How can trust be quantified?
- How can hardware provenance be verified?

## Policy Recommendations

The authors recommend:

1. Strengthening cybersecurity requirements in medical device regulations.
2. Embedding security during device design and manufacturing.
3. Improving supply-chain transparency and traceability.
4. Continuously monitoring cybersecurity risks throughout device lifecycles.

---

## Key Takeaway

The paper argues that the next generation of medical device cybersecurity challenges will emerge not from software bugs alone, but from invisible weaknesses embedded deep within global supply chains.

Ensuring secure healthcare delivery requires a holistic approach combining:

- Secure hardware
- Secure software
- Transparent supply chains
- Lifecycle governance
- International regulation

Medical device cybersecurity must evolve from reactive patching toward proactive, supply-chain-aware resilience.

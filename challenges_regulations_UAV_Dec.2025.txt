# Executive Summary

This paper presents a comprehensive security-centric survey of civilian commercial unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) with a distinctive emphasis on **security regulation and policy enforcement**, rather than only attack detection or mitigation.

Unlike previous surveys that primarily catalog vulnerabilities or defenses independently, the authors propose an integrated framework that systematically connects:

* UAV architecture
* Security threats
* Countermeasures
* Regulatory challenges
* Future research directions

The paper argues that future drone ecosystems require **built-in regulatory capabilities** capable of enforcing security policies in real time, instead of relying solely on manufacturer-defined restrictions or external monitoring.

---

# Motivation

Commercial UAVs are rapidly becoming critical infrastructure for:

* package delivery
* emergency response
* agriculture
* inspection
* surveillance
* healthcare logistics

However, the same characteristics that make drones useful also make them attractive attack targets.

Current commercial drones lack mechanisms allowing governments or trusted authorities to:

* dynamically enforce no-fly zones
* stop compromised drones
* revoke malicious missions
* securely issue mandatory commands

The authors identify this as one of the largest unresolved problems preventing safe large-scale UAV deployment.

---

# Main Contributions

The authors identify two major research gaps:

1. Existing literature does not systematically connect attacks with corresponding defenses.

2. Current UAV architectures provide almost no support for trusted policy enforcement by regulatory authorities.

To address these gaps, the paper contributes:

* comprehensive attack taxonomy
* comprehensive countermeasure taxonomy
* attack-to-defense mapping
* evaluation methodology
* future research roadmap
* proposal for a secure UAV regulatory architecture

# Multi-dimensional Attack Evaluation

Instead of merely listing attacks, every attack is evaluated using six engineering metrics:

* Confidentiality impact
* Integrity impact
* Availability impact
* Severity
* Mission disruption
* Irrecoverability
* Stealthiness
* Exploitation complexity

This enables comparison and prioritization of threats.

# Regulatory Innovation

The paper's most distinctive contribution is its proposal for a **Regulatory Authority (RA)** integrated directly into UAV operations.

Instead of treating regulators as external observers, the authors envision them as trusted participants in the drone ecosystem.

The proposed RA would provide:

* drone registration
* real-time tracking
* dynamic no-fly-zone enforcement
* secure emergency commands
* compliance monitoring
* cybersecurity certification
* blockchain-based audit logging
* AI-driven anomaly detection

---

# Fundamental Research Questions

The authors identify three unresolved questions:

1. How can drones be designed so that regulatory commands cannot be ignored?

2. How can a regulator communicate securely with drones without creating new attack vectors?

3. How can malicious drone behavior be detected automatically and reliably in real time?

These questions form the basis of the proposed research agenda.

# Strengths of the Paper

* Comprehensive end-to-end treatment of UAV security.
* Clear mapping between attacks and defenses.
* Practical evaluation metrics for both threats and countermeasures.
* Strong emphasis on engineering trade-offs rather than theoretical security alone.
* Novel focus on integrating cybersecurity with regulatory policy enforcement.
* Identification of concrete research problems for next-generation UAV systems.

---

# Limitations

The paper is primarily a survey and conceptual framework rather than an implementation study. It does not experimentally validate the proposed regulatory architecture, quantify the performance of the envisioned Regulatory Authority, or provide formal security proofs. The proposed framework remains a forward-looking research agenda whose practical deployment will require trusted hardware support, standardized communication protocols, and regulatory cooperation.

---

# Key Technical Ideas

The paper introduces several important technical ideas that distinguish it from prior UAV security surveys:

1. **Component-centric security analysis**, organizing attacks and defenses by UAV subsystems rather than by isolated attack classes.

2. **Multi-dimensional threat evaluation**, assessing each attack according to confidentiality, integrity, availability, severity, mission impact, irrecoverability, stealthiness, and exploitation complexity.

3. **Attack-to-countermeasure mapping**, enabling direct traceability between specific vulnerabilities and applicable defenses.

4. **Performance-aware security evaluation**, comparing countermeasures in terms of computational cost, memory usage, latency, communication overhead, deployability, implementability, robustness, and usability.

5. **Policy-driven UAV security**, reframing cybersecurity as a mechanism for enforcing operational policies in addition to protecting systems from attacks.

6. **Trusted Regulatory Authority architecture**, proposing a secure entity capable of issuing authenticated commands, dynamically enforcing operational restrictions, monitoring compliance, and responding to malicious drone behavior.

7. **Trusted hardware integration**, advocating the use of Trusted Execution Environments, secure boot, firmware attestation, hardware roots of trust, and secure storage to guarantee that regulatory commands cannot be bypassed.

8. **Future-ready communication security**, emphasizing lightweight authenticated encryption, continuous communication-path verification, secure identity management, and resilient protocols suitable for resource-constrained UAV platforms.

9. **Cross-layer security perspective**, integrating hardware, firmware, operating systems, communication networks, sensors, artificial intelligence, and regulatory policy into a unified security framework rather than treating these layers independently.

Overall, the paper advances the idea that future commercial UAV ecosystems should be designed as **policy-aware, regulation-compliant cyber-physical systems**, where trusted security mechanisms and regulatory enforcement are integral architectural components rather than optional add-ons.

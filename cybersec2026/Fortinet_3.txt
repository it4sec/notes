## Key Points

### 1. Global AI Regulation Landscape
- Major regions (USA, EU, China, Japan) have different approaches to AI governance ranging from risk-based regulation (EU), innovation-first (USA), flexible self-regulation (Japan), to state-led control (China).
- Taiwan aims to "catch up or surpass" global AI regulation, recognizing it must enact national-level AI legislation.

### 2. Taiwan AI Strategy
- Dual engines: Promote innovation and implement ethical governance.
- Establish a legal framework ("AI Basic Law") that balances encouraging technological advancement with protecting rights, managing risks, and ensuring system security.
- The National Science and Technology Council (國科會) will be the lead agency, emphasizing development over pure regulation.

### 3. AI Basic Law Overview
- Acts as a constitutional, high-level framework guiding Taiwan’s AI policies.
- Sets out principles such as human-centric AI, accountability, transparency, privacy protection, digital equality, security, and fairness.
- Future detailed implementing laws ("effect laws") to follow over the next two years.
- Emphasizes AI trustworthiness and responsible innovation.

### 4. Core Ethical Principles (Seven Guiding Principles)
- Sustainable development and welfare.
- Human autonomy.
- Fairness and non-discrimination.
- Accountability.
- Transparency and explainability.
- Information security and system robustness.
- Privacy protection and data governance.

### 5. Security and Compliance Requirements for AI Systems
- Secure AI lifecycle to prevent threats.
- Privacy by design to protect personal data and promote minimal use.
- Transparency to communicate and mark AI-generated content appropriately.
- Clear responsibility and traceability with logs and audit trails.

### 6. Security Challenges of AI Applications
- New attack surfaces emerge with AI agents such as chatbots, creating hidden attack vectors that bypass traditional defenses.
- Example: SQL Injection attacks through AI-chat interfaces, bypassing WAF protections by leveraging natural language inputs.
- Data leakage risks from employees uploading sensitive data to external AI services (e.g., ChatGPT).

### 7. Fortinet’s Security Fabric Solution
- Advocates a holistic, integrated security framework covering networking, cloud services, endpoints, and AI applications.
- Products include FortiAI (Assist, Protect, Secure AI), FortiWeb, FortiGate, FortiDLP, FortiAnalyzer, FortiSIEM, and FortiSOAR.
- Solutions provide automated detection, prevention, audit, and compliance evidence generation.
- Emphasizes proactive, comprehensive AI risk governance rather than reactive patching.

### 8. AI Security Roadmap
- Three phased approach recommended:
    1. Boundary defense: Protect exposed AI APIs.
    2. Internal monitoring: Secure internal AI training environments with zero trust.
    3. Compliance automation: Automate AI incident responses and generate compliance reports.

## Important Conclusions and Insights
- Taiwan’s AI Basic Law sets a timely and balanced framework merging innovation and ethical governance, essential for global competitiveness.
- AI security extends beyond traditional perimeter defenses to cover AI-specific attack vectors and data governance issues.
- AI projects’ success depends heavily on cybersecurity capabilities; security teams become key players.
- Legal and technical compliance must be deeply integrated.
- Fortinet’s Security Fabric aims to translate legal AI principles into actionable technology solutions providing visibility, control, and auditability.
- Trust is positioned as the core asset for sustainable AI-driven business value.

---

## Technical and Other Problems Emphasized in the Presentation

### Technical Problems:
- **AI “Black Box” Transparency:** Deep learning models’ decisions are difficult to explain, posing a challenge for legal and ethical compliance.
- **New Attack Surfaces via AI Agents:** AI chatbots and APIs introduce hidden, natural language input attack vectors allowing injection or command manipulation undetected by traditional WAFs.
- **Data Leakage Risk:** Employees inadvertently leaking sensitive company or customer data to external AI services, creating irreversible data exposure.
- **Comprehensive Governance Complexity:** Need for integrated monitoring, logging, and automated response across diverse AI security controls, data governance, and regulatory compliance.
- **Zero Trust in High-Throughput AI Training Environments:** Managing secure, low-latency AI model training with strict access control.

### Other Challenges:
- **Balancing Innovation and Regulation:** Avoid stifling innovation with excessive regulatory burdens while managing ethical, privacy, and security risks.
- **Legislative Uncertainty:** Basic law provides framework but detailed "effect laws" defining operational compliance measures are forthcoming.
- **Need for Interdisciplinary Coordination:** Collaboration across legal, technical, and operational teams to translate principles into practice.
- **Proactive vs Reactive Security Posture:** Emphasizes shifting from patching known vulnerabilities to building a proactive AI risk governance framework.

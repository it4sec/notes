## Title
**Building a Practical and Compliant LLM Security Chain Using Open Technologies**

## Author/Presenter
- Hua-Rong Qu  
  Senior Researcher, Chunghwa Telecom Research Institute  

## Key Points
1. **Overview of AI Security and Compliance Progress**
   - Prompt injection attacks remain a leading security risk for Large Language Models (LLMs).
   - Important references: OWASP Top 1 for LLM, classical Chinese jailbreak prompt optimization research (ICLR 2026).

2. **Safety Guard Technology**
   - Introduction of system safeguard layers that impose extra security policies on model inputs and outputs to cover risks not fully mitigated by the LLM itself.

3. **Evolution of Security Guard Models**
   - Timeline of LLM-based safety models development:
     - Meta: Llama Guard (Dec 2023)
     - Google: ShieldGemma (Jul 2024)
     - OpenAI: gpt-oss-safeguard (Aug 2025)
     - Alibaba: Qwen3Guard (Sep 2025)
     - MediaTek: Research Breeze Guard (Mar 2026)
     - Chunghwa Telecom: TWGuard (Apr 2026)

4. **Complete Security Compliance Chain**
   - Integration of AI architecture/frameworks, RAG (Retrieval Augmented Generation), agentic workflows, and ongoing data governance processes.
   - Key functions include embedding unstructured data, vector databases for private knowledge storage, reranking relevant content, task planning/execution, external API/tool usage, and continuous self-reflection and evaluation.

5. **Security Risk Cases**
   - Risk 1: Secrets Leakage – accidental exposure of credentials such as passwords, API keys.
   - Risk 2: PII (Personally Identifiable Information) Protection – shielding sensitive personal data from model providers and unauthorized visibility.
   - Risk 3: Hallucination in Agentic Workflows – preventing LLMs from generating inaccurate or fabricated responses that can impact business decisions.

6. **Mitigation Tools and Techniques**
   - Secrets Leakage:
     - Yelp Detect-Secrets: secret scanning, redaction, extensibility for various secret types and configs.
   - PII Protection:
     - Microsoft Presidio: Named Entity Recognition (NER) based PII anonymization compliant with GDPR, HIPAA.
     - OpenAI Privacy Filter: combined model masking PII and secrets with high precision.
   - Hallucination Risk:
     - IBM Granite Guardian: model evaluating context relevance, groundedness, and answer relevance to detect hallucinated claims.

7. **Additional Notes**
   - Discussion on Autonomous AI Agents (e.g., OpenClaw) and associated security risks.
   - Methods for agent threat detection: Rule-based (Agent-Threat-Rules) and LLM-driven pattern analysis by SlowMist.
   - Emphasis on technology-neutral, open-source solutions for constructing compliance and risk mitigation chains in AI systems.

## Important Conclusions and Insights
- Effective AI risk mitigation requires comprehensive protection beyond the model itself, encompassing the entire AI system architecture.
- Open and neutral technical approaches can connect and enhance protective measures across different application domains of AI.
- Balancing AI value delivery and risk control is achievable through systematic implementation of detection, redaction, and grounding mechanisms.
- Early identification and structured defense against prompt injection, secrets leakage, PII exposure, and hallucinations are critical to secure AI deployment.
- Compliance with global and regional privacy regulations (e.g., GDPR, HIPAA) is integrally supported by current anonymization and detection technologies.

## Technical and Other Problems Described in the Presentation

1. **Prompt Injection Attacks**
   - Attackers manipulate prompt inputs to bypass safety rules, causing generation of dangerous or unauthorized content.
   - Recognized as a top security issue for LLMs.

2. **Secrets Leakage**
   - RAG retrieval may expose sensitive fields (passwords, API keys, credentials) due to lack of filtering in massive enterprise data lakes.
   - High cost and operational difficulty in data cleansing and enforcing granular access control.
   - Accidental leakage paths need automated detection and redaction.

3. **PII Exposure**
   - Customer input containing sensitive personal info (IDs, credit card numbers, phone numbers) may be stored in logs or passed to external parties (e.g., annotation vendors).
   - Risks arise when PII is accessible to unauthorized personnel during model training and data pipeline processes.
  
4. **Hallucinations in Agentic Workflows**
   - LLMs may fabricate non-existent product specs or facts when generating outputs.
   - This is dangerous in business-critical contexts like ERP procurement and can cause costly errors.
   - Reliable grounding mechanisms to cross-check responses with source documents are essential.

5. **Performance and Trade-offs**
   - Transformer-based privacy and secret detection models, while accurate, bring higher latency and computational demands.
   - A balance is needed between speed, precision, and scalability for enterprise deployment.

6. **Autonomous AI Agent Risks**
   - OpenClaw-like personal AI assistants with broad system permissions present new threat vectors.
   - Security must consider potential malicious skill execution, prompt injections, and unauthorized data access.

7. **Rule-Based and LLM-Driven Detection Gaps**
   - Rule sets require continuous updates mapped to known CVEs and threat patterns.
   - LLM approaches must handle false positives and confirm actual intent and context of detected threats.
  
---

This presentation advocates building comprehensive, layered, and open AI security and compliance systems that manage evolving risks across data, models, workflows, and deployment environments.

# Summary of Presentation

## Title
How to Manage Vulnerabilities in the Financial Industry?  
From Compliance to Risk-Oriented Strategy

## Author/Presenter
Chen Jiechang  
Chief Information Security Officer  
TS Holdings / Taishin Bank

---

## Key Points

- **Definitions:**
  - Bug (Error): Coding mistakes causing functional or reliability issues, not necessarily security-related.
  - Flaw: Logical errors in system design leading to inherent risks, not coding mistakes.
  - Weakness: Potential security gaps in hardware, software, networks, or systems due to design, implementation, or management deficiencies.
  - Vulnerability: Exploitable security weaknesses allowing unauthorized access, control, or service disruption.

- **Vulnerability Management as a Branch of Risk Management:**
  - Vulnerability management should follow risk management policies.
  - It involves identifying, assessing, prioritizing, and remediating vulnerabilities.
  - Compliance requirements, business continuity, and risk management principles guide vulnerability management.
  - Industry statistics show increasing CVE counts, emphasizing the need for prioritization.

- **Building a Vulnerability Management System:**
  - Use PDCA (Plan-Do-Check-Act) for continuous improvement.
  - Follow frameworks and standards like OWASP OVMG, ISO 29147/30111, NIST SP800-40/216.
  - Employ security models such as PDRR (Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover) and Information Security Continuous Monitoring (ISCM).

- **Core Concepts:**
  - Governance: Define roles, responsibilities, and accountability (Top-Down approach).
  - Strategy: Set objectives based on regulation, standards, business needs, and practices.
  - Life Cycle: Manage vulnerabilities throughout their entire lifecycle.
  
- **Organizational Governance Model (R&R):**
  - Security Committee: Governance and major decisions.
  - CIO & CISO: Oversight and implementation assurance.
  - Operational teams responsible for scanning, patching, and remediation.

- **Compliance and Business Requirements:**
  - Various financial regulations mandate periodic vulnerability scanning, penetration testing, and source code analysis.
  - Requirements include timely patching of critical and important vulnerabilities.

- **Vulnerability Management Life Cycle:**
  - Asset inventory and policy setup.
  - Vulnerability discovery using scanning and threat intelligence.
  - Risk-based vulnerability assessment using frameworks such as CVSS, KEV, EPSS, VISS, and SSVC.
  - Remediation via patches or mitigations.
  - Re-scanning to confirm fixes and detailed documentation.
  - Continuous improvement by evaluating metrics and addressing root causes.

- **SSVC (Stakeholder-Specific Vulnerability Categorization):**
  - Prioritization method to guide patching decisions:
    1. Exploitation status
    2. Automatable mitigation possibility
    3. Technical impact
    4. Mission criticality
    5. Public well-being impact
  - Actions range from “Track” (defer) to “Act” (immediate patching).
  
- **Maturity Model:**
  - Based on SANS Vulnerability Management Maturity Model (VMMM):
    - Levels: Initial, Defined, Quantified, Optimized.
    - Progression from informal to automated and predictive patch management.

- **Maturity Assessment for Continuous Improvement:**
  - Assess across five dimensions:
    1. Strategy and organization
    2. Identification and discovery
    3. Analysis and evaluation
    4. Remediation and management
    5. Metrics and improvement

- **Conclusion:**
  - Through continuous, dynamic, and detailed risk management processes, limited resources can be focused precisely on vulnerabilities that are both highly exploitable and highly threatening.

---

## Important Conclusions and Insights

- Vulnerability management in financial services needs to evolve from merely compliance-driven to risk-driven to effectively reduce security risks.
- A well-structured governance framework with clear roles at all levels improves accountability and results.
- Using quantitative risk assessment models such as SSVC allows prioritizing vulnerability remediation efforts based on business impact.
- Continuous improvement and maturity assessments ensure that vulnerability management adapts to emerging threats and organizational changes.
- Integration of automated tools, external threat intelligence, and metrics provides a comprehensive approach to managing the vulnerability life cycle.

---

## Problems Emphasized in the Presentation

- Increasing volume and complexity of vulnerabilities (CVE counts rising dramatically by 2025).
- Legacy or flawed designs introduce logical errors that cannot be fixed by simple code patches.
- Many vulnerabilities remain unpatched due to lack of risk-based prioritization or organizational oversight.
- Challenges in coordinating cross-departmental remediation efforts.
- Insufficient or informal vulnerability management policies and governance.
- Lack of mature processes for vulnerability identification, prioritization, remediation, and ongoing evaluation.
- Difficulty balancing compliance deadlines and risk-driven priorities.
- Limited resources require precise targeting of vulnerability remediation efforts, focusing on "most likely exploited" and "highest threat" vulnerabilities to maximize security efficacy.

---

This presentation provides a full risk-oriented approach and framework for financial institutions to manage vulnerabilities effectively while aligning with regulatory requirements and business continuity needs.

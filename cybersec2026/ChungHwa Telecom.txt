# Summary of Presentation

## Title
How to Become a Cybersecurity Personnel Who Is Not Disliked – A Guide for Workers to Protect Themselves Well

## Author/Presenter
Artsun Hsieh  
Senior Engineer at ChungHwa Telecom  

## Key Points

- **Introduction & Disclaimer**  
  The presentation shares personal opinions unrelated to the presenter's company’s actual business operations.

- **About the Topic**  
  How can cybersecurity personnel avoid being disliked? How to become a cybersecurity professional who is respected and not a burden within their organization?

- **The Dilemma of Workers in Cybersecurity**  
  Cybersecurity personnel are often caught in the middle between IT and organizational demands, facing continuous conflicts.

- **The Biggest Risk in Cybersecurity is People**  
  According to a diagram, personnel behavior accounts for 90% of the risk, while security governance, zero trust, devices, red/blue teams, penetration testing, vulnerability management, AI, etc., only account for 10%.

- **The Softest Part of Cybersecurity**  
  Cybersecurity workers often receive blame and carry “black pots” (unjust burdens) for issues.

- **Facing Unrealistic Demands**  
  Example demands like “upgrade devices before the end of the workday without service impact” expose challenges of inadequate resources (money, time, staff).

- **How Workers Should Protect Themselves**  
  Reasons for upgrades: reduce breach chances and comply with ISMS & ISO 27001 certification requirements. Risk identification and management must be documented.

- **Avoiding Taking the Fall ("Back-Burning")**  
  Risk assessments should confirm whether action is necessary, and risk treatment plans must be approved by relevant supervisors. Proper resource allocation is essential.

- **The Ideal Worker (“Strongest Worker”)**  
  A worker should be justifiable (meet their obligation to inform), reasonable (request resources accordingly), compliant (pass audits/certifications), and agreed upon (shift risk responsibilities appropriately).

- **When Risk Inventory Is Not Just Homework**  
  Risk inventory should align with actual operations and be realistic, evaluating assets, processes, and risks in a balanced way.

- **Conflict Originates from Communication Gaps**  
  Cybersecurity personnel issue regulation commands, while those “subject to cybersecurity” share responsibilities via risk management, highlighting the need for improved communication.

- **Governance Should Be Like the Wind and Grass**  
  The metaphor implies governance should be strong yet adaptable; steady like an unmoving tree and compliant like moss that listens to the wind.

- **Dedication of the Presentation**  
  To workers stressed by cybersecurity demands and cybersecurity staff who work hard but are not appreciated, hoping improved risk management makes cybersecurity more collaborative and less one-sided.

- **Takeaway**  
  When facing risks: workers should confront, identify, and manage risks; managers should accept residual risk.

- **Contact Information**  
  Provided for further questions.

## Important Conclusions and Insights

- The human element is the largest risk factor in cybersecurity management.
- Cybersecurity personnel often bear unfair blame, requiring careful risk communication and shared responsibility.
- Proper risk assessment and collaborative risk handling involving supervisors and stakeholders are crucial to prevent workers from being scapegoated or pressured unrealistically.
- Resources must be granted adequately to manage cybersecurity risks effectively.
- Governance should be resilient and adaptive rather than rigid and punitive.
- Effective communication bridges the gap between cybersecurity mandates and operational realities.
- Documentation and compliance to ISO 27001 and ISMS standards underpin responsible cybersecurity practices.

---

## Problems and Challenges Highlighted in the Presentation

- **Misalignment of Expectations**  
  Cybersecurity teams often face impossible deadlines and demands without sufficient resources, leading to conflicts and stress.

- **Communication Breakdown**  
  Insufficient communication between cybersecurity personnel and other employees results in misunderstandings and disputes over responsibilities.

- **Blame and Accountability Issues**  
  Cybersecurity professionals, especially frontline workers, frequently become the scapegoats for breaches or failures that are often systemic.

- **Risk Inventory and Management Gaps**  
  Risk assessments are sometimes treated as mere formalities ("homework") rather than practical, reality-based exercises.

- **Resource Constraints**  
  Lack of budget, time, and human resources limits effective security management and upgrades.

- **One-way Security Enforcement**  
  Cybersecurity is sometimes perceived as a unidirectional imposition rather than a cooperative process involving multiple stakeholders.

- **Need for Better Governance Models**  
  Organizations lack flexible, resilient approaches to cybersecurity governance that can adapt to challenges rather than relying on rigid rules.

These problems highlight the need for balanced risk management, adequate resource allocation, improved communication, and shared responsibility to protect both cybersecurity interests and workers’ well-being.

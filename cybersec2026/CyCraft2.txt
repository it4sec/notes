# Summary of Presentation: "Original Sin of Active Directory - Long Live with NTLM Relay"

### Title
Original Sin of Active Directory  
Long Live with NTLM Relay  

### Authors/Presenters
- Jimmy Su (dog lover / gamer / researcher; X: @jimmysured)  
- Presented May 5th, 2026, at CYBERSEC 2026, Taiwan Security Conference

### Key Points
- **Overview of NTLM**: NTLM (New Technology LAN Manager) is an authentication protocol Microsoft introduced in 1993 replacing LAN Manager (LM). It was designed to mitigate replay attacks using challenge-response mechanisms.

- **NTLM Relay Fundamentals**:  
  - Relay is a man-in-the-middle (MITM) attack on authentication where attackers do not need to decrypt credentials but forward authentication messages to another target.  
  - Relay attacks can bypass protections like Signing, MIC (Message Integrity Code), EPA (Extended Protection for Authentication), and Channel Binding (CB).

- **Historical Context**:  
  - Before NTLM Relay became popular, attacks like Pass-the-Hash required direct access to NT hashes. Relay removed the need for hashes upfront.  
  - NTLM Relay attackers exploit protocol transition by forwarding NTLMSSP messages across different protocols (e.g., SMB to LDAP or HTTP).

- **Challenges in Mitigating NTLM Relay**:  
  - Signing and MIC protect message integrity within individual protocols but do not prevent cross-protocol relay attacks.  
  - EPA/Channel Binding aims to ensure the authentication channel matches the intended service to prevent protocol confusion but is often misconfigured or unsupported by third-party apps.  
  - NTLM is still widely used due to legacy systems, third-party software, printers, scanners, and silent fallbacks when Kerberos fails.

- **Kerberos Relay Possibility**: Contrary to common belief, Kerberos authentication can also be relayed or reflected due to flaws and misconfigurations, notably related to SPN (Service Principal Name) and DNS manipulation.

- **Attack Techniques and Combinations**:  
  - NTLM Relay techniques combined with coercion attacks (PrinterBug, PetitPotam, ShadowCoerce) enhance attack capabilities.  
  - Attackers can perform dangerous actions such as LDAP write (DCSync), enrolling fake certificates, SMB share access, and executing remote commands.

- **Defenses and Countermeasures**:  
  - Enable protocol signing on SMB and LDAP (especially requiring signing on server-side).  
  - Enable EPA and strict Channel Binding (set LDAP CBT to "Always").  
  - Disable NTLMv1 and migrate NTLM traffic toward Kerberos.  
  - Disable or restrict unused services like Print Spooler on Domain Controllers and WebClient.  
  - Continuous monitoring using auditing logs for NTLM usage and abnormal certificate issuance events.  
  - Regular use of attack simulation tools for defensive testing.

### Important Conclusions and Insights  
- NTLM Relay remains a critical and hard-to-fix vulnerability in Active Directory environments due to its foundational design and legacy dependencies.  
- Simply disabling NTLM is not trivial because of widespread legacy system dependencies and fallback behaviors.  
- Defenses must be multi-faceted involving server-side enforcement of signing, EPA, channel binding, and close monitoring.  
- Kerberos was once seen as safe from relay attacks but is now proven vulnerable, requiring similar mitigations.  
- Practical defense requires audit enablement, attack surface reduction, and defensive testing with known attack tools.  
- Ongoing vigilance and timely deployment of fixes and policy enforcement are essential to combat relay-based attacks.

## Technical and Other Problems Emphasized in the Presentation

- **Legacy Dependence on NTLM**:  
  - Many third-party software products, old devices (printers, scanners), and legacy shares and SDKs depend on NTLM and cannot easily be replaced.  
  - Silent fallback from Kerberos to NTLM complicates efforts to fully disable NTLM.

- **Protocol Signing Limitations**:  
  - SMB signing only protects SMB traffic; LDAP and other protocols require separate signing enforcement.  
  - Many servers do not enforce signing by default (improvements start with Server 2025 version).

- **Cross-Protocol Relay Problem**:  
  - Signing and MIC do not prevent attackers from forwarding packets across different protocols, leading to relay attacks.  
  - Channel Binding and EPA are designed to fix this but require client and server support, often missing in legacy or third-party apps.

- **Message Integrity Code (MIC) Removal Attacks**:  
  - CVE-2019-1040 demonstrated how MIC can be dropped from authentication messages and still accepted, allowing manipulation of negotiate flags.  
  - Proper deployment requires simultaneously enabling MIC and session signing.

- **Kerberos Relay Reality**:  
  - Assumed secure, Kerberos can be relayed or reflected due to weaknesses in SPN handling and DNS.  
  - CVEs such as 2025-33073 and 2026-24294 highlight these issues.

- **Coercion Attacks “Combo”**:  
  - Attacks using coercion to force victims to authenticate (such as PetitPotam) combined with relay attacks greatly increase exploitation reach.

- **Monitoring & Visibility Gaps**:  
  - Difficulties in identifying who is still using NTLM or misconfigured channels.  
  - Need for enhanced logging, event collection, and analysis (e.g., NTLM events, unsigned LDAP bind events, AD CS abnormal events).

By understanding and addressing the above systemic, protocol, and operational challenges, organizations can better protect Active Directory environments from the persistent threat posed by NTLM and Kerberos relay attacks.

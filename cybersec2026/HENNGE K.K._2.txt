# Summary of Presentation

## Title
Pets or Cattle? A Cautionary Tale

## Authors/Presenters
Simon McFarlane, Cloud Infrastructure Engineer

## Key Points

1. **Stuxnet Overview**
   - A highly expensive and critical cyberweapon designed to disable uranium centrifuges at the Natanz nuclear facility in Iran.
   - The Stuxnet attack exploited stolen driver signing certificates and a chain of vulnerabilities to infect specific industrial control systems.

2. **Cybersecurity Threats - Evil Maid Attack**
   - A scenario illustrating insider threats where cleaning staff with physical access to devices can compromise security despite strict key control policies.
   - Shows that even well-guarded physical security can be subverted via human factors.

3. **The Concept of Pets vs. Cattle in IT Infrastructure**
   - On-premise servers (pets): Individually configured, manually maintained, patched, and monitored servers.
   - Cloud servers (cattle): Managed centrally, ephemeral, taking tasks from a pool without persistent states, and equipped with advanced mitigations.
   - The presentation advocates for modern cloud-based infrastructure ("cattle") over traditional on-premise ("pets") to reduce vulnerabilities.

4. **System Trust and Threats**
   - Questions regarding what is actually running on a system, with emphasis on hidden spyware, rootkits, malicious kernel modules, and compromised firmware in hardware components like GPUs, motherboards, and SSDs.
   - Challenges of verifying firmware and kernel-level security.
   - Highlights the risks of using vulnerable hardware accessories and threats posed by Direct Memory Access (DMA) via PCI-E and Thunderbolt interfaces.

5. **Recommendations for Security**
   - Importance of booting from network with read-only filesystems.
   - Use of stateless clusters and central datastores to avoid persistent local states.
   - Remote attestation for machine trust verification.
   - Adoption of zero-trust principles and excellent cloud security to thwart ordinary attackers.
   - Monitoring insider threats with Data Loss Prevention (DLP) tools such as HENNGE.

6. **Geopolitical Cybersecurity Insights**
   - BlackTech, a cyber actor linked to the People's Republic of China, modifies router firmware undetectably.
   - Exploits domain trust relationships to pivot attacks from overseas subsidiaries to headquarters in Japan and the U.S.
   - Mitigation measures are recommended for detection and prevention of such advanced persistent threats.

7. **Cost Comparison**
   - Stuxnet development cost estimated at $100 million (2024 dollars).
   - A single Stuxnet attack equated to roughly 22 hours of firing U.S. Tomahawk cruise missiles, which cost about $3.6 million each and take up to two years to build.

## Important Conclusions and Insights

- Trust in physical and on-premise systems is increasingly tenuous due to sophisticated insider and hardware-based attacks.
- Cloud infrastructure, when implemented with zero-trust and centralized management principles, offers stronger security guarantees.
- Firmware-level attacks are complex and difficult to detect without physical inspection, representing a critical risk in hardware.
- State-level actors use highly sophisticated methods to penetrate trusted networks; ordinary users and companies should avoid being vectors for such attacks.
- Monitoring tools are necessary but insufficient if the foundational trust in devices and infrastructure is compromised.
- Modern cybersecurity strategies must adapt to address both sophisticated advanced persistent threats (APT) and everyday adversaries by embracing cloud technologies and stringent security controls.

# Technical and Other Problems Highlighted

- **Firmware and Hardware-Level Security Challenges**
  - Difficulty in verifying legitimate firmware versus maliciously altered firmware.
  - Potential for malware to hide within kernel modules or firmware, evading detection by monitoring tools.
  - Risks from hardware accessories like GPUs that can be leveraged to pivot attacks into system memory.

- **Threat of Direct Memory Access (DMA) Attacks**
  - Devices connected via PCI-E or Thunderbolt ports can be exploited to gain unfettered memory access, bypassing OS security controls.
  
- **Physical Security vs. Cybersecurity**
  - Insider threats such as the "Evil Maid Attack" illustrate how physical access, even by trusted third parties like cleaning staff, can compromise networks.
  
- **Legacy On-Premise Infrastructure Vulnerabilities**
  - On-prem servers as "pets" require labor-intensive maintenance; their individual configurations can create inconsistent security postures.
  - Loose procurement processes for sensitive hardware increase risks of supply chain attacks.
  - Once adversaries gain access to the network from physical entry points, they have ample opportunity to exploit vulnerabilities.

- **Challenges in Attribution and State-Level Threats**
  - State-level actors use sophisticated, stealthy tactics that evade standard detection and exploit trust relationships across international boundaries.
  
- **Cost Effectiveness of Cyberattacks vs. Physical Attacks**
  - Extremely high costs and logistical demands for kinetic weapon deployment highlight the relative efficiency and attractiveness of cyber operations like Stuxnet.

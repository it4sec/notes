# Summary of Presentation on AI Security & Safety

## Title
**The First Security Issue for Enterprises Introducing AI: How to Balance Innovation and Data Protection**

## Author/Presenter
Ray Pai  
Forcepoint  
Asia-Pacific Technical Consultant (Taiwan, Korea, Japan)

## Key Points

- **AI Tool Explosion:**  
  Over 18,000 AI products available on G2 platform with rapid growth, making manual tracking impossible. It would take 17 years to test all tools at a rate of trying three per day.

- **Current Market Data and Risks:**  
  - 69% of enterprises detected employees using banned GenAI tools.  
  - Gartner predicts 40% of enterprises will face Shadow AI (unapproved AI tool usage) security or compliance incidents by 2030.  
  - IBM estimates an additional $650k loss per data breach caused by Shadow AI.  

- **Real Enterprise Case Studies:**  
  - Microsoft Copilot used by over 6,599 employees at broad organizational scale.  
  - Engineers renting GPUs for AI workloads without IT knowledge (RunPod).  
  - High-risk external AI tools like PDF.ai and openclaw.ai processing sensitive data outside the company.

- **Visibility and Control:**  
  Enterprises lack full visibility and control on five key AI data transmission channels:  
  1. Web Browser  
  2. Endpoint AI applications  
  3. M365 / Copilot platform  
  4. Enterprise internal GenAI APIs  
  5. Direct third-party AI APIs

- **False Choices: Blocking vs. Full Access:**  
  - Full access delays governance and exposes data to third parties, lacks audit trails, and responsibility cannot be outsourced.  
  - Total blocking provides an illusion of security, cannot keep pace with AI tool growth, and drives usage underground beyond corporate control.

- **Recommended Approach: The Third Way:**  
  Implement governance at data flow points through:  
  - Visibility: Real-time detection of AI tool usage, actors, and data content.  
  - Policy enforcement: Granular, risk-based controls rather than blanket bans, allowing approved tools with conditions and intercepting risky actions immediately.  
  - Auditability: Full event records and data provenance to reconstruct data flows and responsibilities during incidents.

- **Governance Must be Sustainable:**  
  Policy frameworks should keep pace with AI innovation using continuous updates and behavior-based dynamic adjustments, allowing general use without disruption and tightening controls on risky behavior.

- **Actionable Recommendations:**  
  1. Conduct an AI app discovery to identify all AI tools in use (including unauthorized Shadow AI).  
  2. Identify largest uncovered risk channels and prioritize coverage (e.g., Web Browser, M365 Copilot).  
  3. Extend existing security frameworks such as DLP and web policy to include AI governance.

- **Business Impact:**  
  Investing in AI security governance reduces compliance risks, shortens incident response times, preserves company reputation, and enhances employee productivity. AI governance will become a procurement evaluation standard by 2026.

## Important Conclusions and Insights

- AI security governance is not about cost but enabling trustworthy and productive AI adoption.  
- Enterprises must avoid extremes of full blocking or unfettered access and focus on balancing innovation with data protection.  
- Real-time visibility, policy enforcement, and auditability form the cornerstone of an effective AI governance framework.  
- Shadow AI represents a significant, ongoing risk that cannot be ignored.  
- Governance must be integrated across all AI data transmission channels to be effective.  
- Preparedness today will reduce costly compliance incidents and reputational damage tomorrow.

## Technical and Other Problems Emphasized

- **Rapid AI Tool Emergence:**  
  AI tools evolve faster than IT teams can block or evaluate them, leading to blind spots.

- **Shadow AI:**  
  Unauthorized use of AI tools leads to uncontrolled data exposure and risk of compliance breaches.

- **Insufficient Visibility:**  
  Existing security tools often lack adequate coverage over endpoints, APIs, and cloud applications.

- **Over-Privileged Access:**  
  Employees often have broader access to AI tools and data than necessary, increasing lateral risk.

- **Data Boundary Ambiguities:**  
  AI capabilities can access unclassified sensitive documents, exposing confidential information inadvertently.

- **Lack of Audit Trails:**  
  Security incidents are difficult to analyze post-facto due to missing data provenance or usage logs.

- **Governance Gaps:**  
  Current policies often lag behind tool deployment speed, leaving compliance and control gaps.

- **Cross-Channel Policy Enforcement Complexity:**  
  Coordinated policies across browsers, endpoints, and cloud platforms are mandatory but complex to implement.

- **Employee Behavior:**  
  Employees circumventing controls (e.g., using personal devices or accounts) undermine IT governance efforts.

- **Data Leakage to Third Parties:**  
  Sensitive business and customer data may enter free public AI models without control or recourse.

---

This presentation underscores the urgent need for enterprises to adopt proactive, integrated AI governance frameworks that provide real-time visibility, conditional controls, and audit evidence to manage AI-related risks effectively — making AI innovation both safe and compliant.

# Summary of Presentation

## Title
**AttackGPT: It Can Talk the Kill Chain — But Can It Afford To Walk It?**

## Authors/Presenters
- Jan Michael Alcantara  
  Threat Researcher, Netskope Threat Labs  
  Presented at Attacking AI Forum, CYBERSEC 2026

## Key Points
- **Research Focus:** Evaluating the ability of Large Language Models (LLMs) to generate and deploy malware in real-time using prompt-based code synthesis.
- **Malware Use Cases Tested:** Infostealer and MedusaLocker ransomware.
- **LLM Models Tested:**
  - Commercial: Opus 4.5, GPT-5.0, Gemini 3.0
  - Open-weight: Qwen2.5-coder:7b, DeepSeek-r1:70b, Llama3:8b
- **Research Objectives:**
  1. Technical Effectiveness: Can LLMs generate functional malware payloads on demand?
  2. Operational Reliability: Can LLMs deliver payloads that maintain the attack chain without human intervention?
  3. Economic Feasibility: Is AI-driven malware synthesis cost-effective?
- **Prompt Methodology:** Use of role-based personas, consistent prompting across LLMs, modular prompts targeting PowerShell scripts as outputs.
  
## Important Conclusions and Insights
- **Infostealer Malware:**
  - LLMs can effectively generate infostealer payloads on demand (Effectiveness up to 95% for Opus 4.5).
  - Almost all LLMs show 100% operational reliability for deploying infostealers directly to victims.
  - Economically viable to synthesize infostealers using AI, with payloads per $1 USD ranging up to 930 (Llama3.1).
- **MedusaLocker Ransomware:**
  - LLMs currently cannot fully generate an effective ransomware payload on demand (Best effectiveness at 54% for GPT-5).
  - Deployment is operationally reliable, especially with open-weight LLM models scoring perfect operational scores.
  - Economic feasibility for ransomware synthesis is mixed and "depends" on the model used—payloads per $1 USD vary widely (3 to 243).
- **General Findings:**
  - Malware containing only prompts is currently feasible for common malware like infostealers but not for complex malware such as ransomware.
  - Gemini 3.0 has the strongest guardrails limiting malicious code generation.
  - Using open-weight models with sample code prompts improves results.
  - Newer LLM versions do not necessarily produce better malicious code; effectiveness depends on the use case.

# Technical and Other Problems Highlighted by the Presentation

- **Malware Complexity:**  
  While infostealers are relatively straightforward for LLMs to generate, complex malware like targeted ransomware remains a challenge in terms of generation effectiveness.

- **Dependence on LLM Model and Configuration:**  
  Effectiveness and operational reliability vary significantly by model, with commercial and open-weight models showing different strengths and weaknesses.

- **Guardrails and Content Moderation:**  
  Strong guardrails in models like Gemini 3.0 can limit the synthesis of explicitly malicious code, requiring attackers to switch models or prompt strategies.

- **Operational Reliability Challenges:**  
  Some generated malware scripts may require human intervention or fixing to maintain continuity in the attack chain.

- **Economic Viability:**  
  While infostealer synthesis is generally economically viable, ransomware synthesis viability depends heavily on the LLM used and the complexity of tasks.

- **Attribution Difficulties:**  
  Just-in-time malware generation through prompts creates bespoke, polymorphic code without malicious files on disk, making attribution and detection difficult for traditional static indicators of compromise (IOCs).

---

This presentation provides a thorough exploration of the current capabilities and limitations of LLMs in generating and operationalizing different types of malware, emphasizing the practical challenges for attackers and defenders alike.

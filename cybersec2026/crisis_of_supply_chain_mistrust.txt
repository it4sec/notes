## Quick Summary

The presentation argues that modern Security Operations Centers (SOCs) are failing because they rely on fragmented tools, siloed teams, and human-intensive investigation processes that cannot keep pace with AI-enhanced threats.

Traditional SOC, MDR, EDR, and XDR solutions generate overwhelming numbers of alerts, forcing organizations to disable features, lower detection sensitivity, and ignore low-priority signals. This creates dangerous blind spots that sophisticated attackers exploit.

AIShield proposes a **Forensic AI SOC** model based on **Agentic AI**—an autonomous AI system that not only analyzes alerts but also performs investigations, gathers evidence, conducts memory forensics, correlates intelligence, and delivers actionable conclusions.

The core objective is to protect the organization's **"golden response time"** by automating deep forensic analysis and reducing the burden on human analysts. 

---

## Goal of the Presentation

The presentation seeks to:

* Expose structural weaknesses in current SOC and MDR operating models.
* Demonstrate the limitations of rule-based alert triage.
* Highlight the operational risks created by alert overload.
* Advocate for forensic-driven security operations.
* Introduce Agentic AI as the next evolution of SOC automation.
* Improve digital trust across supply chains and external ecosystems.
* Strengthen cyber resilience through faster detection, investigation, and response.

## Key Ideas

### 1. Multiple Security "Brains" Create Fragmentation

The presentation argues that modern enterprises operate multiple disconnected security systems:

* SOC
* MDR
* EDR
* XDR
* SIEM
* Cloud security platforms

Each solution generates separate alerts and workflows, creating:

* Responsibility gaps
* SLA failures
* Communication breakdowns
* Vendor finger-pointing

The speaker refers to this as the **"stacking myth"**—adding more tools does not necessarily improve security outcomes. 

---

### 2. Alert Fatigue Forces Organizations to Accept Risk

According to the presentation:

* Organizations receive over **10,000 alerts per day**.
* Analysts review less than **5%** of alerts.
* Many organizations enable only around **50%** of purchased security capabilities.
* Fewer than **10%** of generated alerts receive meaningful investigation.

As a result, organizations:

* Lower detection sensitivity
* Disable security modules
* Ignore informational alerts
* Miss early indicators of compromise

This creates hidden attack paths for advanced adversaries. 

---

### 3. Vendors Deliver Verdicts, Not Forensics

Most security providers focus on:

* Classifying threats
* Closing tickets
* Sending notifications

However, defenders actually need:

* Attack timelines
* Kill chain reconstruction
* Attribution analysis
* Lateral movement tracking
* Persistence detection

The presentation argues that **deep forensics—not alert triage—is the true bottleneck in security operations.** 

---

### 4. AI Assistants Are Not Enough

The speaker distinguishes between two AI approaches:

| AI Assistant             | Agentic AI                      |
| ------------------------ | ------------------------------- |
| Answers questions        | Executes investigations         |
| Provides recommendations | Collects evidence automatically |
| Requires manual actions  | Operates across systems         |
| Generates tasks          | Produces conclusions            |

The presentation emphasizes that organizations need AI systems with both:

* Intelligence ("brain")
* Execution capabilities ("hands")

This enables autonomous evidence collection and analysis. 

---

### 5. Forensic AI SOC Principles

The proposed model is based on five principles:

1. **Single team, single responsibility model**
2. **100% investigation coverage**
3. **Full utilization of security tools**
4. **Delivery of conclusions instead of task lists**
5. **True automation without manual playbooks**

The guiding philosophy is:

> One Brain · One Team · One Outcome. 

---

### 6. Investigation Is the Real Time Sink

The presentation highlights that:

* Detection is relatively fast.
* Response can be automated.
* Investigation remains slow and labor-intensive.

The speaker argues that the security industry focuses too heavily on detection technologies while underinvesting in automated forensic investigation capabilities.

## Case Studies and Practical Examples

### Case 1: Early Warning Signals

Machine-learning-based behavioral baselines identified attack indicators days before high-priority alerts were generated.

However, analysts often ignore these signals because of excessive false positives and limited resources. 

---

### Case 2: Low-Risk Alerts Concealing APT Activity

An alert classified as informational involved:

* `MicrosoftEdgeUpdate.exe`
* Legitimate-looking process chains
* No malicious file hash indicators

Memory forensics later revealed:

* Fileless malware execution
* Cobalt Strike activity
* Attribution to APT41

This demonstrates why low-priority alerts require deeper investigation. 

---

### Case 3: Long-Term Intrusion Discovery

During a LockBit ransomware investigation, analysts uncovered evidence of a separate intrusion that had persisted since 2022.

Key findings:

* APT41 persistence mechanisms
* Dustpan malware components
* Cobalt Strike implants
* 42 months of dwell time

The case illustrates the value of retrospective forensic analysis. 

---

### Case 4: Impossible Travel Detection

Traditional validation required approximately:

* 30 minutes per alert
* Multiple manual workflows
* User outreach
* Log analysis

Agentic AI reduced investigation time to:

* Approximately 30 seconds per alert

The system automatically:

* Correlated login records
* Evaluated device context
* Assessed MFA status
* Generated final recommendations

This demonstrates substantial gains in response speed.

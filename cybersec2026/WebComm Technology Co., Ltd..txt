# Summary of Presentation

## Title
When AI Can Take Any Role: Facing New Challenges in Product and Supply Chain Security (當AI能轉生成任何角色 面對產品與供應鏈安全新挑戰)

## Authors/Presenters
- Fiona Cheng, Senior R&D Manager at WebComm

## Key Points
- In 2026, AI can play various roles in product engineering including customer support, product/project management, software engineering (junior to mid-level), and vulnerability research.
- Large Language Models (LLMs) are already capable vulnerability researchers, finding issues in Firefox and the Linux Kernel without complex design or special training.
- AI-powered vulnerability research enables automatic identification of software weaknesses but may suffer from high false-positive rates and inconsistent report quality.
- There is a significant shift in the balance of offense and defense in cybersecurity; attackers can rapidly weaponize vulnerabilities while defenders face longer and more complex remediation workflows.
- Security must evolve with AI’s adoption by controlling risks, managing resources effectively, and improving decision-making including “using magic against magic” (i.e., employing AI to combat AI-driven attacks).
- Four strategic entry points for handling AI challenges in security:
  1. Strategy - defining risk tolerance and AI adoption levels with human oversight to minimize fatigue.
  2. Consensus - establishing shared security definitions, internal verification, and external agreements (contracts/SLA).
  3. Execution - accelerating and improving patch accuracy using AI for vulnerability prioritization, automated testing, and verification.
  4. Prevention - supply chain management and security-by-design principles, enhanced with AI-assisted threat modeling and real-time risk monitoring.
- AI security introduces new attack surfaces requiring control of shadow AI, identity and permission governance, and traceable AI behavior.
- Emphasizes acting pragmatically rather than spreading fear; offers a stepwise action plan to assess risk tolerance, inventory critical open source dependencies, map vulnerability-to-patch timelines, and explore suitable acceleration/improvement methods for security posture.

## Important Conclusions and Insights
- AI will permeate the entire software supply chain, introducing risks spanning from open source dependencies to product delivery.
- The core challenge is not the volume of issues but prioritizing resources on the right problems.
- Vulnerability discovery is becoming easier but more noisy, increasing remediation costs and necessitating novel defense strategies.
- Attackers gain speed via AI-enabled weaponization, making the defense process—dependent on human decisions and coordination—relatively slower and more difficult.
- Establishing a comprehensive AI adoption and risk management strategy is critical to maintaining security in an era of rapid AI advancement.
- Collaboration within and outside organizations, combined with AI-enabled automation balanced by human oversight, is key to effective vulnerability management.
- Proactive prevention and a shift-left mindset (fixing issues early in development) are essential for reducing cyber risk.
  
---

## Technical or Other Problems Emphasized
- **AI-driven vulnerability discovery leads to higher false positives:** Increased noise can overwhelm security teams, requiring better triaging and prioritization mechanisms.
- **Skewed offense-defense balance:** AI accelerates attackers’ ability to exploit bugs, while defenders must navigate a complex, multi-stage patching and validation process that is slower and human-intensive.
- **Quality of vulnerability reports:** Early AI-generated reports, especially for critical bugs like SSRF, may lack quality or require high privileges to exploit, posing difficulties in rapid and accurate risk assessment.
- **Resource management:** Controlling costs and risk appetite becomes challenging as AI scales the number of identified security issues.
- **Security model changes:** Open ecosystems can no longer guarantee safety without new controls as AI shifts the threat landscape.
- **Shadow AI and AI governance:** Ensuring responsible AI use requires mechanisms for managing unauthorized AI tools, controlling AI identity and permissions, and enabling traceability and rollback for AI actions.
- **Human fatigue and oversight necessity:** Continuous human validation is necessary to prevent errors, but fatigue must be minimized to sustain effective workflows.
- **Dependence on open source and critical infrastructure:** Organizations face difficulties controlling external risks and must carefully monitor and manage third-party dependencies.
- **Need for advanced AI-assisted tooling:** Growing complexity demands AI support in producing SBOM/VEX data, vulnerability analysis, automated testing, and patch prioritization.

---

This presentation provides a holistic view of how AI transforms cybersecurity in product engineering and supply chains and urges proactive, balanced approaches combining human judgment and AI automation to address emerging risks effectively.

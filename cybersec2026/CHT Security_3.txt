# Summary of Presentation

---

## Title
**Ecological Joint Defense: Cross-Agency Intelligence Sharing and Collaborative Defense**

---

## Author/Presenter
Chen Jiechang  
Chief Information Security Officer  
Taishin Shin Kong Financial Holdings / Taishin Bank

---

## Key Points

- Traditional cybersecurity methods such as "castle-style defense" are no longer sufficient alone.
- Cyber threats have evolved from targeting single organizations to ecosystems, emphasizing the need for collective defense strategies.
- Cyberattacks increasingly exploit shared suppliers, highlighted by cases such as SolarWinds and Korean Leaks, showing supply chain vulnerabilities.
- The AI era introduces more complex threats, necessitating collaboration between large banks and regulators internationally.
- Effective ecological joint defense requires trust and mutual benefit among participants, including:
  - Establishing supplier security corridors with risk-based segmentation.
  - Demanding Software Bill of Materials (SBOM) to quickly locate risks.
  - Revising contractual terms for security responsibilities and data protection.
  - Cross-border and cross-organizational joint scenario exercises.
  - Integrating international frameworks such as NIST CSF and FIRST for standardized cooperation.
  - Enhancing SOC (Security Operations Center) maturity and leveraging SOAR (Security Orchestration, Automation, and Response) to automate high-frequency, low-risk alerts.
  - Sharing threat intelligence and managing vulnerabilities collaboratively.
- Transitioning from competition to cooperation is emphasized to reduce blind spots in defense.
- Challenges in cross-agency cooperation often lie in "willingness" and "standards," not just technical issues.
- Promoting standardization of languages like STIX/TAXII ensures timely and automated machine-to-machine sharing of threat intelligence.
- De-identification and privacy techniques are crucial to overcoming the reluctance to share sensitive internal incident information ("keeping family shame inside").
- Emphasizing automation (SOAR workflows) to rapidly respond to threats; manual transfer of intelligence (e.g., Excel sheets) is insufficient.

---

## Important Conclusions and Insights

- Cybersecurity defenses must evolve from isolated organizational silos toward an interconnected ecosystem defense.
- Trust, transparency, and accountability underpin effective collaborative cyber defense.
- International cooperation and adherence to global standards significantly enhance response speed and coverage.
- Automation and integration of threat intelligence are essential in coping with the speed and scale of modern cyber threats.
- The biggest hurdles to collaborative security are organizational and cultural rather than purely technological.
- Sharing of anonymized threat intelligence can build collective resilience without exposing sensitive internal issues.

---

## Technical/Other Problems Highlighted

- Supply chain attacks exploiting vulnerabilities in common suppliers (e.g., SolarWinds hack and Korean Leaks operations).
- Challenges in information sharing across agencies due to lack of common standards and reluctance to share sensitive data.
- Need for standardized, machine-readable formats for threat intelligence exchange (e.g., STIX/TAXII).
- Low maturity and automation levels in many SOCs leading to inefficiency in high-volume alerts.
- Difficulty in rapid identification and tracing of attack chains without integrated intelligence systems.
- Psychological barriers in disclosing internal security incidents ("family shame") that limit transparency.
- Manual threat intelligence sharing methods that hinder timely and effective responses.

---

This presentation stresses the criticality of a joint defense ecosystem fueled by trust, automated intelligence sharing, supply chain vigilance, and international collaboration to tackle the increasingly sophisticated cyber threats in the AI era and beyond.

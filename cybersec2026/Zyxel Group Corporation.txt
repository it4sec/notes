## Key Points

### 1. Overview of CRA and Market Importance (Slides 3-8)

- **CRA** (Cyber Resilience Act) is a new European Union market regulation focusing on products with digital elements, including hardware, software, firmware, digital components, and remote maintenance/support capabilities.
- Product safety can no longer rely on post-incident remedies; it must begin with design and lifecycle governance.
- Compliance with CRA is a market entry requirement in 2026, emphasizing brand trust, global market access, and supply chain competitiveness.
- Taiwan manufacturers should not treat CRA as a European-only regulation but recognize its importance in global business qualifications.
- The true risks go beyond fines to include product delisting, recalls, channel disruptions, and customer order shifts.

### 2. Core Abilities Required for 2026-2027 EU Market Stability (Slide 4)

Three key capabilities define success:

1. **Quick judgment and reporting** (2026 immediate capability) — PSIRT, reporting, SBOM (Software Bill of Materials)
2. **Ability to scale compliance processes** (2027 scaling capability) — processes, evidence, supply chain
3. **Sustained customer trust** — product portfolio, brand, long-term maintenance.

### 3. CRA Governance and Compliance Roadmap (Slides 9-19)

- Prioritize product governance by high risk and EU market impact.
- Classification of products determines evaluation paths and depth.
- CRA demands cross-department product governance processes involving roles such as product line management, R&D, PSIRT, QA/testing, procurement/supply chain, legal/compliance, and customer service.
- Speed of vulnerability notification (24/72 hours) is critical, requiring adequate preparation.
- SBOM must be accurate, maintainable, and queryable to serve as a real-time decision support database.
- CRA requires governance from product design, release, and ongoing maintenance phases, not just pre-shipment checks.
- Compliance documents (TCF, DoC, CE marking) constitute an evidence chain proving continuous product safety.
- Supply chain security is vital; trusted components are essential for trusted products.
- Start compliance efforts with a single product line and build scalable, repeatable templates.
- Blueprint for 2026-2027 compliance:
  - 2026: Build immediate capability (PSIRT, SBOM, vulnerability judgment, reporting).
  - 2027: Scale and institutionalize processes (Documentation, CE marking, lifecycle governance).
  - Beyond 2027: Cultivate global customer trust linked to product safety value chain.

## Important Conclusions and Insights

- Compliance is **not a cost**, but a **license to compete** in the global market.
- Product safety has evolved from a cybersecurity demand into a core **commercial qualification**.
- Failure to comply threatens not only financial penalties but also the **loss of market access and customer trust**.
- A disciplined, cross-functional, lifecycle-oriented approach to product safety is essential.
- Maintaining accurate, detailed, and timely SBOM data is crucial for managing risk and compliance.
- Establishing a repeatable compliance model starting with one product line mitigates risk and accelerates scaling.
- Trust cultivated through compliance becomes a market advantage beyond mere regulatory adherence.

---

## Technical or Other Problems Described or Emphasized in Presentation

- **Scaling vulnerability risk:** Single device weaknesses, once exploited at scale (as seen in the Mirai botnet case), can cause global market shocks.
- **Need for proactive design security:** Product safety requires front-loaded effort in design and development to avoid exploitable weaknesses.
- **Cross-departmental coordination challenges:** CRA demands that governance and remedial action span organizational boundaries and roles.
- **Difficulty ensuring SBOM quality:** Producing an SBOM is not enough; it must be precise, up-to-date, verifiable, and accessible as a decision tool.
- **Rapid reporting pressure:** Firms must develop the ability to accurately assess and report security incidents within narrow time windows (24-72 hours).
- **Supply chain transparency:** Trust in final products hinges on trusted components and supplier communications, requiring robust tracking and disclosure of third-party parts.
- **Complex classification and compliance pathways:** The diversity of product types (general, class I/II important, critical products) complicates compliance routes and documentation standards.
- **Managing long product lifecycles:** Safety obligations extend beyond launch to include sustained monitoring, updating, patching, and verification.

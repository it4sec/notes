### Key Points

1. **Current Landscape of Cybersecurity in Taiwan's Public Companies**  
   - Most companies with capital above NT$10 billion disclose cybersecurity measures in their annual reports (approx. 115 companies).  
   - Cybersecurity disclosures are increasing in depth and breadth, with an average of 2.4 pages dedicated to cyber efforts in reports as of 2024.  
   - Common disclosed cybersecurity practices include appointing Chief Information Security Officers (CISO), establishing dedicated security teams, and achieving ISO 27001 certification.  
   - Gaps remain in adoption of advanced defense measures such as zero trust, business continuity management, XDR/MDR threat detection, and cybersecurity insurance.

2. **Shift from Compliance to Resilience**  
   - Regulatory frameworks are evolving from compliance-driven approaches (audit and check) to resilience-driven models emphasizing sustained operational continuity and rapid recovery post-attacks.  
   - Critical performance indicators like MTTD (Mean Time to Detect), MTTR (Mean Time to Respond), RTO (Recovery Time Objective), and RPO (Recovery Point Objective) become key metrics.  
   - AI integration is viewed as a necessary strategic element due to its dual role as both a threat vector and a defense tool.

3. **AI and Cybersecurity**  
   - AI is not merely a tech trend but a strategic cybersecurity issue: used increasingly by attackers (e.g., >80% of phishing attacks employ AI), introducing new vulnerabilities through AI-generated code, and requiring AI governance policies.  
   - Anthropic’s Claude Mythos Preview exemplifies AI’s advanced role in automatic vulnerability discovery and exploitation, calling for a new paradigm in cybersecurity talent and operations leveraging AI for defensive purposes.

4. **Regulatory Environment and Trends**  
   - Taiwan’s cybersecurity laws and security management practices are rapidly evolving with a focus on mandatory disclosures, incident reporting timing, and supply chain risks.  
   - Influential regulations parallel global trends including NIS 2, DORA, and SEC Item 106 emphasizing board accountability, resilience testing, supply chain inclusion in security management, and strict incident reporting timelines.  
   - The Financial Supervisory Commission (FSC) in Taiwan is enforcing stronger regulations with doubled inspection rates and expanding criticality definitions for incident reporting.

5. **Incident Response and Loss Management**  
   - Companies are advised to build capabilities in crisis response through rehearsals, to understand and calculate losses (financial, operational, reputational), and apply legal frameworks on ransomware payments and reporting.  
   - Collaboration with government cybersecurity investigation units and industry alliances is stressed for rapid reaction and mitigation.

6. **Use of Incentives and Tools**  
   - Taiwan’s Industrial Innovation Act allows tax deductions for investments in cybersecurity products and services, encouraging companies to modernize and enhance defenses financially.  
   - Companies should leverage this incentive alongside increased internal preparedness and proactive threat hunting.

7. **Recommendations and Best Practices**  
   - Compliance is the baseline, not the goal. Resilience requires ongoing capabilities and measurable outcomes validated through board-level governance and continual testing.  
   - AI must be integrated into cybersecurity policies as a core element, with dedicated governance and reaction mechanisms starting 2026 for all public companies.  
   - Organizations should collectively upgrade defenses through industry-wide cooperation, shared learning, and joint investment in advanced capabilities.

---

### Important Conclusions and Insights

- **Cybersecurity disclosure is increasingly detailed but still lags in advanced threat detection and supply chain security.**  
- **Resilience is replacing compliance as the dominant paradigm, demanding fast detection, response, and recovery capabilities rather than just paperwork.**  
- **AI is a transformative game-changer in cybersecurity, representing both an elevated threat and essential defensive asset.**  
- **Regulators globally and in Taiwan emphasize governance at the board level, standardized incident reporting deadlines, and including supply chain in oversight.**  
- **Collaboration, rehearsals, and incentives are necessary to elevate cybersecurity posture and reduce ransomware impact.**  
- **Taiwan’s cybersecurity regulation is not static but rapidly evolving with major updates expected by 2025 focusing on resilience and strict enforcement.**  

---

### Problems and Challenges Highlighted by the Presentation

- **Governance gaps:** Board participation in cybersecurity remains insufficient despite regulatory emphasis.  
- **Technical gaps:** Many companies lack adoption of critical technologies like zero trust architecture, advanced detection (XDR/MDR), and continuous monitoring.  
- **Incident reporting and transparency:** Disclosures often lack detailed insight into incident impact and losses, while reporting thresholds are still evolving.  
- **Supply chain security:** Oversight of subsidiaries and third-party providers remains weak, posing systemic risk.  
- **Cybersecurity workforce and culture:** While education and phishing exercises are widespread, advanced offensive/defensive exercises (red/blue/purple teams) and certifications lag.  
- **Ransomware response dilemmas:** Difficulties in balancing payment decisions with incident recovery, regulatory compliance, and disclosure requirements.  
- **AI integration challenges:** Managing AI-generated vulnerabilities, governance, and the dual-use nature of AI in attacks and defense add complexity not yet fully addressed.  
- **Regulatory complexity and pace:** Companies face challenges keeping abreast of the rapidly shifting regulatory environment and harmonizing it with operational practices.

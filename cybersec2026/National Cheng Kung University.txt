## Key Points

1. **Three+ Core Messages**  
   - Proposes a computational approach to understanding complex systems, including both software and hardware.  
   - Systems are continuously evolving.  
   - Emphasizes security-centric [Inter-]Agentic, Physical, and Cyber AI.  
   - Highlights the spectrum between open-sourced AI models and adaptive open standards.  
   - Computational ethics and human issues are acknowledged but not covered in detail due to time constraints.

2. **System Complexity**  
   - Modern systems, such as those built by Intel and Nvidia, are significantly complex.  
   - Example: NVIDIA Vera Rubin NVL72, a supercomputer integrating multiple CPUs, GPUs, DPUs, and high-speed networking technologies.  
   - OpenBMC as an example of platform management using object-oriented programming and thinking, with protocols like PLDM (Platform Level Data Model) and Redfish.

3. **AI Challenges for Systems**  
   - AI vs AI competition inevitable, with LLMs (Large Language Models) still insufficient for system-level understanding.  
   - Transformers designed for sequential NLP tasks, but interaction between system components may require concurrent, structural handling of data (e.g., JSONs, graphs).  
   - Concept of LEM (Large Executable/Explainable Model) introduced as a next step beyond LLMs.

4. **Inter-Agent Understanding**  
   - Systems or components should ideally understand each other computationally.  
   - Increasing agentic AI capabilities require APIs to either share intentions or conceal them.  
   - This mutual understanding enhances trustworthiness and could address OT (Operational Technology) security challenges.

5. **Open vs Closed Models**  
   - Recognizes the impracticality of full open-sourcing in complex systems.  
   - Necessity to manage a balance between open standards and proprietary models.  
   - Proposes semi-stable APIs (like PLDM, Redfish, Omniverse) for gradual update cycles.  
   - Envisions more advanced “negotiating” APIs enabling dynamic agent interactions.

6. **Future Security Landscape**  
   - Anticipates numerous new security challenges emerging from the interaction of evolving AI, system complexity, and open/closed model dynamics.  
   - Mentions CYBERSEC 2036 as a speculative timeframe for advanced cybersecurity evolution.

7. **Practical Use Case**  
   - Showcases a smart campus lighting system using SecureML AI DevOps Platform integrating PQC (post-quantum cryptography), backdoor defense, and secure communication.  
   - Highlights programmable APIs, AI transparency, user privacy, and “Internet-less” IoT to strengthen trustworthy AI.

---

## Important Conclusions and Insights

- The complexity of modern cyber-physical systems demands computational methods that go beyond traditional software and hardware boundaries.  
- Trustworthy AI in OT security situations depends on better agentic AI collaboration and mutual understandability.  
- The evolution from LLM to LEM signals a transition towards executable and explainable models that interact intelligently with system components.  
- A mixed ecosystem balancing open-source and proprietary models combined with adaptable, negotiable APIs is critical.  
- Future work in OT security needs to focus on agentic AI negotiation, adaptive executable models, and addressing emergent security risks anticipated through 2036 and beyond.

---

## Technical and Other Problems Emphasized

- **System Understanding and Complexity**: Difficulty in fully grasping internally coordinated but fragmented subsystems (e.g., OpenClaw issue).  
- **LLM Limitations**: LLMs are not sufficient for system-level reasoning; designed primarily for natural language processing, not deep system interactions.  
- **Token Probability and Model Transparency**: Challenges in verifying model output consistency and understanding internal decision-making mechanisms.  
- **Component Awareness**: Uncertainty if components should know operational contexts (e.g., virtualization environments, firewall rule changes).  
- **API Stability vs Negotiation**: Need to balance semi-stable APIs with the requirement for dynamic negotiation and adaptation between agents.  
- **Security Risks from New Technologies**: Growing surface area for attacks with emerging AI capabilities and increasingly complex hardware/software ecosystems.  
- **Trust, Privacy, and Ethics**: Though ethics and human issues are not extensively covered, the presentation acknowledges the importance and complexity of these challenges in security and AI.

This summary encapsulates the presentation’s vital elements on OT security challenges, AI’s role in evolving systems, and the path forward for trustworthy, adaptive, and secure cyber-physical systems.
